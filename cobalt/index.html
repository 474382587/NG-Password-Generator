Company Profile

Canada Cobalt Works Inc. (CCW) is a Canadian-based junior mining enterprise focused on new discoveries and cobalt extraction at its past producing Castle mine in the Northern Ontario Cobalt Camp. A team with deep roots in the district combined with extensive geological, metallurgical and engineering expertise, has allowed Canada Cobalt to become the first company in the region to gain critical underground access and produce a premium grade cobalt sulphate product. In addition, CCW is drilling aggressively for new discoveries underground at Castle, where Cobalt was historically ignored.

Castle Mine was one of the highest-grade silver-cobalt properties in the historic Cobalt Camp in Northern Ontario.

Mines in the greater Cobalt Camp (including Gowganda, Cobalt, Silver Centre) produced over 500 million oz of silver and over 30 million lbs of cobalt in the 1900s

Reasons Canada Cobalt Works Inc. is Your Next Smart Move

Canada Cobalt Works Inc. Is Bringing The Dead Back To Life.

From the early 1900’s to the late 1980’s, the Castle mine was a major contributor to regional production that made Gowganda-Cobalt-Silver Centre in northeastern Ontario one of the richest silver mining regions on earth. Documented production exceeded half a billion ounces of silver, propelled by extraordinary grades.

The initial silver boom in this metal endowed area in the early 1900’s was so spectacular, it outmatched the Klondike Gold Rush in many respects and vaulted Canada into a premier position on the world mining stage.

Silver prices would fluctuate over the decades, surging to an all-time high of $50 (U.S.) per troy ounce in 1980.(1) That’s when Agnico Eagle put the Castle and other mines back into production in northeastern Ontario.

By the early 1990’s, however, silver was trading at a bottom-feeding $3.58 per troy ounce, (2) and some of the mining communities in northeastern Ontario, including Gowganda, Cobalt and Silver Centre, quickly turned into ghost towns.

With silver prices so low, it was impossible to make any money from a silver mine, and operations throughout northeastern Ontario, including Castle, were shuttered.

But the mining of silver over a period of about eight decades also included scooping 30 million pounds (~14,000 tonnes) of cobalt, demonstrating potential for extraction of this metal in this part of Canada. Much of that cobalt was actually used for Canada’s war efforts during the first half of the 20th century. Beyond that, however, cobalt’s uses at the time were rather limited, and mining it presented serious challenges due to low prices and metallurgical issues.

Despite their potential to produce large quantities of the now much-needed metal, at much higher than average global grades, northeast Ontario’s silver mines and prospective cobalt ground sat idle until just recently.

Frank Basa looked over the situation more than a decade ago and saw enormous opportunities. He was convinced that one day the demand for cobalt would soar, and as a metallurgical specialist he was confident he could find a solution to recovery issues.

Basa was part of northeast Ontario’s silver mining boom, and the Agnico Eagle team in the 1980’s, and he remembered how the cobalt was so evident underground. Yet the miners would show only scant interest in the metal – the cobalt “bloom” that was so prevalent was used primarily as an indicator to vector toward the highest-grade silver! Cobalt was mostly treated as waste product, and arsenic in the ores complicated recoveries for any metals beyond silver.

Basa knew the best properties that Agnico Eagle held, and came to the realization that the Castle silver mine was actually a “cobalt gold mine”!

Castle offered plenty of “cobalt bloom”, where the cobalt is visible to the naked eye, and Basa knew that he had to acquire this property and some others. That’s exactly what he did.
